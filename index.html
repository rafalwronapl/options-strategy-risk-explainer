Model Assumptions

European Black-Scholes approximation for mark-to-model scenarios.
Per-leg IV is supported, but no volatility smile/skew surface is inferred.
Dividend yield is modeled as a continuous yield approximation.
No borrow costs, commissions, slippage, or broker margin model.
American-style early assignment risk is described as a warning, not priced.

Methodology Boundary

Expiry payoff risk is calculated from entered legs at expiration and assumes the underlying price cannot go below zero.
Mark-to-model scenarios are estimated with Black-Scholes and are not maximum-loss or maximum-profit limits.
Liquidity warnings use manually entered bid, ask, and open interest. Missing or stale market data can invalidate execution conclusions.
Spread cost uses full bid/ask width across entered option legs as a conservative friction indicator, not as expected slippage.
Broker margin and assignment are warnings only. The prototype does not calculate broker-specific requirements.

Mini-Lessons: How To Read The Output

What should I check first?

Start with maximum loss, breakeven, tail risk, and liquidity warnings. Greeks matter, but they can create false confidence if the payoff and tail are not understood first.

Why can short strangles be dangerous?

A short strangle can collect small premiums while markets are calm, then lose quickly in a large move. The central part of the chart may look stable while the real danger sits in the tail.

What does theta not tell me?

Theta estimates time decay. Positive theta does not mean the position is safe. Short premium can have positive theta while also carrying negative gamma and large move risk.

Why do bid/ask and open interest matter?

Theoretical payoff can look attractive, but wide bid/ask spreads or low open interest can erase edge and make exits difficult.
